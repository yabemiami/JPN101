Less. 2.1: Deictic Pronouns これ・それ・あれ : げ P60

Deictic independent pronouns: これ・それ・あれ
Japanese does not have the 2-way deictic system as in English (this/that, here/there). It has a 3-way system instead: this one / that one / that one over there.
  • Closer to the speaker: これ · this one
  • Closer to the addressee: それ · that one
  • Away from both: あれ · that one over there
Example
  1. これは わたしのほんです。This (one) is my book.
  2. それは やまださんのほんですか。Is that (one) Yamada-san's book?
  3. あれは たなかさんのほんです。That (one over there) is Tanaka-san's book.
Your own: point at three objects and describe with これ・それ・あれ.

Less. 2.2: Deictic Determiners この〜・その〜・あの〜 : げ P61

Deictic determiners: この〜・その〜・あの〜
The 3-way deictic system: [この Noun] / [this Noun]; [その Noun] / [that Noun]; [あの Noun] / [that Noun over there].
  • Closer to speaker → modify with この〜
  • Closer to addressee → modify with その〜
  • Away from both → modify with あの〜
Example
  1. [このほん]は わたしのほんです。This book is my book.
  2. [そのほん]は やまださんのほんです。That book is Yamada-san's book.
  3. [あのほん]は たなかさんのほんです。That book over there is Tanaka-san's book.

Less. 2.3: Deictic Locatives ここ・そこ・あそこ : げ P62

Deictic locatives: ここ・そこ・あそこ
The 3-way deictic system: ここ · here; そこ · there; あそこ · over there.
  • Closer to speaker → ここ · here
  • Closer to addressee → そこ · there
  • Away from both → あそこ · over there
Example
  1. ここは 日本にほんです。Here is Japan.
  2. そこは トイレといれです。There (close to you) is the restroom.
  3. あそこは カナダかなだです。Over there is Canada.

Less. 2.1–3: Interrogative どれ・どの・どこ

Grammatical Construction
The three sets of deictic expressions have an interrogative counterpart: どれ/which one; [どの NP]/[which NP]; and どこ/where.
Example
  • どれが やまださんのくるま ですか。Which one is Yamada-san's car?
  • どのくるまが やまださんのくるま ですか。Which car is Yamada-san's car?
  • やまださんのへやは どこですか。Where is Yamada-san's room?
Note
The suffix 〜が is a "focus" marker. It is used with interrogative phrases, while 〜は cannot mark interrogative phrases (cf. げ P63).

Less. 2.4: だれ/who and だれの/whose : げ P62

Grammatical Construction
だれ means who. By adding 〜の to it, we derive the possessive whose 〜 = だれの〜, in the same way that わたし/"I" turns into わたしの〜/"my〜".
Example
  1. あのひとは だれ ですか。Who is that person?
  2. あのくるまは だれのくるま ですか。Whose car is that?
Note
だれ and だれの〜 can appear in the subject position:
  • だれが にほんじん ですか。Who is Japanese (here)?
  • だれのくるまが プリウスですか。Whose car is a Prius?

Less. 2.5: Noun-も

Grammatical Construction
Noun-も marks an expression that shares a property already mentioned in the context, like English "too".
Example
やまださんは にほんじん です。Yamada-san is a Japanese national.
たなかさんも にほんじん です。Tanaka-san is also a Japanese national.
Note
〜も must be added directly to the part that shares the aforementioned property. This is unlike English "too," which can appear at the end of a sentence: I am American too. vs. I, too, am American.
Your own: find a classmate who shares a property with you and report with も.

Less. 2.6: A is not B : げ P63

Grammatical Construction
じゃありません / ではありません / じゃないです are the negative counterparts of です: A is not B.
Example
  • わたしは にほんじん です。 I am Japanese.
  • わたしは にほんじん じゃありません。 I am not Japanese.
  • わたしは にほんじん ではありません。 I am not Japanese.
  • わたしは にほんじん じゃないです。 I am not Japanese.
Note
ではありません sounds a little more formal than じゃありません, while じゃないです sounds less formal and more colloquial.

Less. 2.7: Conversational particles 〜ね and 〜よ : げ P64

Grammatical Construction
〜ね and 〜よ can be added at the end of a sentence to convey how it is used in conversation. 〜ね is like English "... right?", which seeks agreement; 〜よ is like "I'm telling you ...", which emphasizes the statement.
Example
  • やまださんは にほんじん ですね。Yamada-san is Japanese, right?
  • やまださんは にほんじん ですよ。Yamada-san is Japanese (I'm telling you).
Note
〜ね and 〜よ are always placed at the end of a sentence.
Your own: write one ね-sentence and one よ-sentence.

カタカナ: Long Vowels & Foreign Sounds : げ P26

1. Long vowels in katakana
The dash ー indicates a long vowel: ア / アー = a / ā; キャ / キャー = kya / kyā; バタ / バター = bata / batā; タキ / ターキー = taki / tākī.
2. Foreign sounds
Combinations of katakana letters write sounds not native to Japanese:
  • ヒュ — ヒューイ /Hyuui/ (Heuy)
  • フュ — フューチャー /fyuuchaa/ (future)
Your own: transcribe three loanwords (e.g., coffee, computer, hamburger) in katakana.
