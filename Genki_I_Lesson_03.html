Verbs 1: Dictionary Forms: る-Verbs \& う-Verbs

Two Conjugational Types:
Verbs in Modern Japanese are grouped as る-verbs or う-verbs in terms of their conjugations (except the two irregular verbs, する \& くる).
Example
る-Verbsう-Verbsru-Verbsu-Verbs
たべるのむtaberunomu
ねるよむneruyomu
おきるはなすokiruhanasu
みるきくmirukiku
みせるいくmiseruiku
かけるかえるkakerukaeru
Note:
  1. Some [...る]-verbs are う-verbs as かえる. The /r/ part of such う-verbs are actually a part of the base: かえる/[kaer]u vis. かける/[kake]ru
  2. Verbs that have /a,u,o/ before the terminal る (.../a,u,o/-る) are all u-verbs while most of the verbs with the .../i,e/-る terminal are ru-verbs with some exceptions (like かえる).

Verb 2: Polite-Present: aff. 〜ます and neg. 〜ません

Grammatical Construction:
VSTEM＋ます・ません: たべます・たべません/のみます・のみません
Polite Form affirmative 〜ます and negative 〜ません are interpreted as present/future expressions.
Example
  1. トムは たべます/Tom will eat.
  2. トムは のみます/Tom drinks.
  3. トムは します/Tom does.
  4. トムは きます/Tom comes.
  5. トムは たべません/Tom won't eat.
  6. トムは のみません/Tom doesn't drink.
  7. トムは しません/Tom doesn't do.
  8. トムは きません/Tom doesn't come.
Note:
Memorize Japanese verbs in Dictionary AND Polite Form. When a [...る]-verb is given, you will be able to tell if it is a る- or う- verb:
かえる/かえります(う/go home) vis-à-vis かえる/かえます(る/change)

Case Particles 1: [Noun]+を, accusative, direct object

Grammatical Construction:
Japanese case particles are a suffix. 〜を is an accusative marker; it indicates that its (host) noun is a direct object of a sentence.
Example
  1. 私わたしは コーヒーを 飲のみます。
    I drink coffee.
  2. ボブは コーヒーを 飲のみません。
    Bob does not drink coffee.
  3. トムは 寿司すしを たべます。
    Tom eats Sushi.
  4. 私わたしは 寿司すしを たべません。
    I do not eat Sushi.
Note:
  1. The accusative marker is the only occurrence of を in Modern Japanese.
  2. It is pronounced as /o/ just as お.

Case Particles 3.2: [Noun]+に, time of an event

Grammatical Construction:
〜に marks a time of an event .
Example
  1. 日曜日にちようびに 大阪おおさかに いきます。
    I will go to Osaka on Sunday.
  2. 八時はちじごろ(に) 大学だいがくに 行いきます。
    I go to University at around 8:00.
  3. 十時じゅうじに ねます。
    I go to bed at 10 o'clock.
  4. 何時なんじに ねますか。
    What time do you go to bed?
Note:
[time]-ごろ means `about', and, with 〜ごろ, 〜に becomes optional.

Time Reference 2: usually without 〜に

Grammatical Construction:
〜に can be added to time-of-an-event expressions to emphasize:
(1) parts-of-a-day expressions: 朝あさ/the morning, 夜よる/the night etc.;
(2) 週末しゅうまつ/weekend
Example
  1. 朝あさ(に) テレビを みます。
    I watch TV in the morning.
  2. 週末しゅうまつ(に) どこに いきますか。
    Where do you go on the weekend?
Note:
Usually these expressions are not suffixed with 〜に unless the time phrase is emphasized.

Case Particles 4: [Noun]+へ, direction/goal of an movement

Grammatical Construction:
へ marks a goal/direction of an movement just as diretctional 〜に (cf. 3.1)
Example
  1. わたしは 今日きょう 学校へ 行いきます。
    I go to school today.
  2. 山田やまださんは 日本にほんへ 帰かえりません
    Yamada-san don't go back to Japan.
Note:
  1. 〜へ as a case particle is pronounced as /e/ as え.
  2. 〜へ can only replace directional/goal 〜に. It cannot replace, for example, temporal 〜に.
  3. 〜へ is more formal than 〜に.. It is often used in the written language.

Idiomatic 〜ませんか: Invitation, ``why don't you/we ...?''

Grammatical Construction:
Present Negative expressions as 〜ませんか can be used to invite someone to do something.
Example
  1. 週末しゅうまつ、ゲームを しませんか。
  2. いっしょにtogether ごはんを たべませんか。
  3. Response:
    1. いいですね。- Sounds great!
    2. う〜ん、ちょっと。。。 - Umm, its slightly .... (inconvenient ...)
Note:
It has to be present-negative, the affirmative counterpart, 〜ますか, can be merely a question: たべますか。/Do you eat?

Japanese, a free-word-order Language

Grammatical Construction:
Japanese is flexible on its word order. Although a subject (if pronounced) is usually sentence-initial and a predicate, a verb or an adjective, sentence-terminal, it's what a speaker wants to emphasize that determines the word order of the rest.
Example
  1. 私わたしは 今日きょう 家うちで 本ほんを 読よみます。
  2. 私わたしは 家うちで 本ほんを 今日きょう 読よみます。
  3. 私わたしは 本ほんを 今日きょう 家うちで 読よみます。
  4. 私わたしは 今日きょう 本ほんを 家うちで 読よみます。
Note:
Even the subject of a sentence can be preceded by another phrase:
今日きょう 本ほんを 家うちで 私わたしは 読よみます。
However, let us keep things simple.

Frequency Adverbs

Grammatical Construction:
Frequency adverbs can be added to a sentence, usually after the subject to indicate frequency of the event/action: 毎日まいにち/everyday; よく/often; ときどき/sometimes. Also, there are ぜんぜん/never and あまり/(not) so that must be used in a negative sentence.
Example
  1. 私は ときどき 喫茶店きっさてんに いきます。
    I go to a coffee shop sometimes.
  2. 私は 毎日まいにち ラーメンを たべます。
    I eat ramen everyday.
  3. 私は よく すしを たべます。
    I eat sushi often.
  4. 私は ぜんぜん 喫茶店きっさてんに いきません。
    I don't go to a coffee shop at all.
  5. 私は あまり 喫茶店きっさてんに いきません。
    I don't go to a coffee shop very often.
Note:
Unlike English never, ぜんぜん and あまり does not make sentences negative by themselves: I never eat sushi. vs. 私は ぜんぜん すしを たべません.

The Topic Particle: 〜は

Grammatical Construction:
So far, we have only used 〜は with the subject. `X〜は' literally means `As for X, it is such that ...' However, 〜は can put a topic on most of the nouns in a sentence.
Example
  1. 肉にくをは たべません。- As for meat, (it is such that) I don't eat.
  2. 明日あしたは 何をしますか。- As for tomorrow, what do you do.
  3. 大阪おおさかには いきません。- As for (to) Oosaka, I will not go.
  4. 図書館としょかんでは マンガは よみません- As for Manga and in a library, I don't read.
Note:
  1. 〜は replace 〜を while 〜は is suffixed to other case particles (e.g., 〜には, 〜では, etc.)
  2. When you have to translate into English, it is not always easy to translate topic interpretations. keep your translation as natural as possible.

How to use くる/いく in Japanese (come/go)

Grammar:
In Japanese, the location of the speaker determines the selection of くる or いく. If a movement is towards the speaker's location, くる is always selected, away form the location いく is selected. In English, the onus of decision can shift.
Example
Japanese
Tel A: パティーに きますか。
Tel B: はい。いきますよ。
English
Tel A: Are you coming to the party?
Tel B: Yes, I am coming.

Idiomatic ちょっと

Grammatical Construction:
ちょっと literally means a little. However, it can idiomatically functions as a polite (or indirect) negative response.
Example
  • Lit. ちょっと 待まってください。- Wait a little!
  • Lit. ちょっと ください。- Please give me a little!
  • Idi. A: 明日あした、いっしょに 映画えいがを みませんか
    Why don't we (go out and) see a movie tomorrow.
    B: 明日あしたは、ちょっと。
    Tomorrow is a little (inconvenient).
Note:
Being indirect is decent or polite in Japan.
